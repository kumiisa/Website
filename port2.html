Data & Information Systems

Explore the foundations of data, information, and the systems that transform raw facts into actionable insights

Data

Raw, unorganized facts that lack context or inherent meaning on their own. These can include numbers, symbols, text, observations, or images.

  • Low-level knowledge measured in bits and bytes
  • Can be quantitative (numeric) or qualitative (descriptive)
  • Not directly useful until processed
Example: Individual student test scores: 85, 92, 78, 88

Information

The result of processing and organizing data into a meaningful form. It adds context and structure to data, transforming it into something useful.

  • Adds context and meaning to raw data
  • Supports analysis and decision-making
  • Presented in interpretable formats
Example: The class average is 85.75, showing strong performance

Key Differences

Purpose

Data is independent and unprocessed, while information is dependent on data and structured to convey meaning

Context

Data lacks context (e.g., "10"), while information provides it (e.g., "The temperature is 10°C today")

Usability

Data doesn't directly aid decisions, while information is specifically used to support decision-making

What is an Information System?

An information system is an integrated set of components designed to collect, process, store, and distribute data and information. These systems support decision-making, coordination, and control within organizations.

Hardware

Physical devices and equipment

Software

Programs and applications

Databases

Organized data storage

Networks

Telecommunications infrastructure

People

Human resources and expertise

Procedures

Defined processes and workflows

Purpose & Importance

Streamline Workflows

Essential tools for optimizing daily operations and supporting strategic planning

Analyze Data

Present insights in a structured way to support impactful decision-making

Drive Innovation

Enable companies to forecast trends, improve customer service, and adapt to market demands

Human & Ethical Aspects

While technology plays a critical role, people and ethical considerations are equally important. Professionals must understand not only how to design and operate these systems but also assess their impact on society, privacy, and user behavior.

The effective use of information systems requires collaboration between people and machines, with careful attention to how data is gathered, interpreted, and used responsibly.

Types of Support Systems

Click on any system to learn more about its features and applications

Transaction Processing Systems (TPS)

▼

Handle routine, high-volume business transactions quickly and accurately

Users: Operational-level employees and managers
Examples: Point-of-sale systems, payroll processing, order entry, inventory management
Key Features: Real-time or batch processing; updates databases; ensures data integrity

Office Automation Systems (OAS)

▼

Automate routine office tasks and support communication

Users: Clerical staff, administrative workers, all employees
Examples: Microsoft Office Suite, email systems, word processors, document management
Key Features: Increases productivity and supports documentation, communication, scheduling

Knowledge Work Systems (KWS)

▼

Facilitate creation and sharing of new knowledge

Users: Engineers, designers, analysts, researchers
Examples: CAD, virtual reality systems, financial modeling workstations
Key Features: Support innovation, design, research, and development activities

Process Control Systems

▼

Monitor and control physical processes in industrial environments

Users: Operational and industrial managers
Examples: Assembly lines, petroleum refineries, food production controls
Key Features: Automate and improve accuracy in manufacturing and production

Management Information Systems (MIS)

▼

Convert data from TPS into meaningful, structured reports

Users: Middle managers and departmental heads
Examples: HR systems, inventory reports, budgeting systems, sales analysis
Key Features: Monitor performance, track operations, assist in tactical decision-making

Decision Support Systems (DSS)

▼

Analyze complex data sets for non-routine decision-making

Users: Middle and senior managers, analysts
Examples: Financial forecasting, logistics planning, "what-if" analysis platforms
Key Features: Interactive tools, simulations, and models to guide decisions

Executive Support Systems (ESS)

▼

Provide senior executives with strategic-level insights

Users: C-level executives (CEO, CFO, CIO)
Examples: Real-time dashboards showing KPIs, market trends, risk analysis
Key Features: Facilitate strategic planning and high-level decision-making

Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)

▼

Integrate core business processes across departments

Users: Organization-wide (finance, HR, logistics)
Examples: SAP, Oracle ERP, Microsoft Dynamics
Key Features: Eliminate data silos, ensure consistency, streamline operations

Customer Relationship Management (CRM)

▼

Manage interactions and relationships with customers

Users: Sales, marketing, customer support teams
Examples: Salesforce, HubSpot, Zoho CRM
Key Features: Enhance customer experience, improve retention, enable personalization

System Hierarchy

Each system plays a unique role within an organization. Some focus on operational efficiency (TPS, OAS), others target tactical decision-making (MIS, DSS), while some support strategic planning (ESS, ERP).

Understanding their distinctions and applications helps organizations leverage the right tools to meet their goals more effectively.
